Fall Detection Patterns

The following list of motion patterns are presented as described in the Fall Detection Pendant Internal Specifications (MHP8002-005).

Real Fall Patterns

301 - Forward Fall

The forward fall is typical of how someone might fall when tripping while walking in a forward direction. It is characterized by the forward movement, a sudden loss of balance, and a sharp face-forward fall.

302 - Sideways Fall

The sideways fall is typical of how someone might fall if they are standing still and lose their balance. It is characterized by falling sideways and striking on your hip and landing on your side. Possible variations include rolling to your face or backward after the fall.

303 - Straight Down Fall

The straight down fall is typical of how someone might fall when standing or taking a step on a bad knee. Imagine a sharp pain that accompanies a buckling. It is characterized by a quick sudden jerk down and the collapsing to your side.

304 - Slow Fall

The slow fall is typical of how someone might fall when falling due to a cardiac arrest, stroke, or other health anomaly. It is characterized by a slow downward fall, possibly grabbing or clutching to furniture in the decline and then an eventual impact to side or face.

305 - Bending Down Fall

The bending down fall is typical of how someone might fall when leaning over to tie a shore or pick up something lying on the ground. It is characterized by a near horizontal torso (facing down) while standing or squatting, at least 5 seconds, then losing balance and falling forward.

306 – “Seizure” Fall

The seizure fall is typical of how someone might fall when falling due to an unexpected seizure. It is characterized by a sudden downward fall, followed by seizure movement lying on the floor for at least 10 seconds.

307 – Slip and Fall

The slip and fall is typical of how someone might fall when falling due to stepping on a slippery surface and losing balance. It is characterized by a quick backward fall, and possibly accompanied by the attempt of grabbing or clutching to furniture, and then an eventual impact to hips.

308 – Falling out of a chair

The falling out of a chair is typical of how someone might fall when falling due to an off balance from getting up from a sitting position. It is characterized by a slight tilted torso (facing down) while getting up, then losing balance and falling forward.

309 – Falling out of a bed

The falling out of a bed is typical of how someone might fall when falling due to an off balance from a lying position. It is characterized by a horizontal torso (facing up or sideways) while lying down, then rolling out of the bed and falling.

310 – Falling down the stairs

Use a dummy or anything to simulate a person and roll it down a few stairs then a flight of stairs.

311 – Sliding out of a chair

The sliding out of a chair is typical of how someone might fall when sitting in a wheel and have gradually reclined to the point that their buttocks slips first out of the chair. It is characterized by reclining and slipping from the chair. The user should end horizontally.

312 – Restricted 301

Repeat motion pattern 301 with a restricted pendant so that it does not dangle freely around the neck. Tuck it inside a tight fitting shirt or restrict it with a strap.

313 – 18” Ramp Drop

Slide down the ramp from an 18 inch position.

314 – Straight drop (from desk)

Drop the pendant straight down onto short nap carpet from the height of a standard desk ~30inches.

Non-Fall Patterns

201 – 10 Steps

Pause for 2 seconds, take 10 steps, then pause.

202 - Jogging

10 seconds of jogging, then slow down to walking.

203 – Walking

Continuous walking.

204 – Laying

Continuous laying on bed (or sofa).

205 – Stairs

Take 6 steps downstairs then 6 steps upstairs

206 – Horizontal movements

horizontal movements in bed or on a mat, like trying to roll over or adjust in bed.

207 – Sitting on sofa

Continuously sitting on sofa reading or watching TV.

208 – Movements on sofa

Leaning over to pick up a paper or remote etc. while sitting.

209 – Sitting down on kitchen chair

Sitting down on a kitchen chair (or some hard surface without any cushion).

210 – Getting into Bed

Climbing into bed either by sitting down hard or slowly climbing in.

211 - Lying down on a sofa

The lying down on a sofa pattern is as it sounds. The trick is to “hop” onto the sofa – with as much force or impact as possible without exceeding a “reasonable” level that would be experienced by a normal movement pattern.

212 – Getting into a recliner

The getting into a recliner pattern is how someone with less mobility might get into a recliner. The movement is characterized by putting your hands on either armrest to support your weight, slowly lowering yourself to about six inches above the seat cushion, then “plopping” into the chair, followed by quickly moving to a full recline position.

213 – Bending down

The bending down pattern is as it sounds. The pattern is characterized by a near horizontal torso (facing down) while standing or squatting. Imagine leaning over to tie your shoes.

214 – The Quick up and back movement

The quick up and back movement is characterized reclining in your chair – far beyond what is considered normal or good posture – almost supine. Then occasionally lean forward abruptly to take a drink, and then recline quickly again.

215 – Getting in and out of sofa

Sitting down on a sofa normally pause then stand up from a sofa normally.

216 – Walking with a walker

Use a walker and do a slow walk for 15 seconds.

217 – Getting in a sofa using walker

Use a Walker to sit down on a sofa very slowly then sit still.

218 – Getting out of a sofa using walker

Use a Walker to get up from sofa very slowly then stand still.

219 – Jumping Jacks

Continuous jumping jacks.

220 – Sit-ups

15 seconds of sit-ups as intense as you can achieve.

221 – Car Seatbelt on

Get into a car, and then buckle up the seat belt.

222 – Car Seatbelt off

Unbuckle the seat belt, get out of a car.

223 – Removal of pants or skirts

How are we going to tackle that one? – Maybe without videotaping.

224 – General Bumping

Wear BC or pendant and bump it either with the edge of table or hand. (No need for all participants, CMO can just record several).

225 – Putting on

Putting the BC on a belt or pants or place pendant around your neck then pause.

226 – Taking off

Taking the BC off a belt or pants (maybe wiggle some) or removing from neck. Place it either on a hard surface or hand it over to someone.

227 – Unplugging

Unplug the BC from the battery charger.

228 – General Handling

This one would be any movement for handling the BC or pendant, such as transferring form one person to the other or picking up and looking at it via different rotations.

229 – Stairs (Up)

Walk up stairs (at least 8 steps)

230 – Stairs (Down)

Walk down stairs (at least 8 steps)

231- 6” Ramp Drop

Hold the pendant 6” above a pad and let go of it.

232-Chair Swivel

Sit in a swiveling chair and rotate 90° left and right twice.

233- Spinning Pendant

Hold the pendant by the lanyard and wrap around 4 times, slowly pull lanyard tight and let pendant rotate until it stops.
